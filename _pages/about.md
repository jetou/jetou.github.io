I am a Ph.D. candidate in Computer Science at Old Dominion University. I have completed my coursework,
passed both the written and oral comprehensive examinations, and advanced to candidacy.

My doctoral research is in **AI for physics** — applying machine learning to scientific inference problems
in hadronic physics. Recent work uses diffusion models to recover physical distributions from raw
scattering events and to perform active inference on inverse problems where the available prior information
is incomplete.

Before starting the Ph.D., my research was in **combinatorial optimization**: learning-based methods for
constraint satisfaction and for large-scale knapsack problems.

## Teaching

Alongside research I have taught undergraduate computer science at Old Dominion for three semesters, and I
am currently seeking an **adjunct faculty appointment** teaching one course per semester, in the Hampton
Roads area or online.

The habit I keep coming back to is this: *produce the problem before introducing the machinery.* In CS 471
the concept students found hardest was concurrency, because a race condition is invisible — the code looks
correct and usually runs correctly, until one time it does not. Abstract explanations of critical sections
did not land. What worked was having students run a deliberately broken counter a thousand times and watch
the output drift. Once they had seen the bug, mutexes stopped being vocabulary and started being a fix for
something real.

The other thing I have learned is that the students who are struggling are usually the ones who do not say
so. Office hours were where most of the actual learning happened in the courses I supported, so I reach out
to quiet students early rather than waiting for them to ask.
